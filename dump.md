According to Viet Nam’s National Strategy for
Natural Disaster Prevention, Response and
Mitigation to 2030, Vision to 2050, storms, floods,
droughts, and saltwater intrusion were identified as
national priorities (Vietnam’s Prime Minister, 2021).
These hazards also strongly affect agricultural
production. The most prominent issues in the MRD
are sea level rise, increased rainfall, extreme events
(e.g., storms, heavy rain, drought, floods), increased
temperatures, and salinity intrusion (CGIAR, 2016).
Because of sea level rise and climate change, the
MRD is experiencing more severe, longer lasting,
and more frequent droughts and more severe and
frequent salinity intrusion during the dry season.
According to agricultural experts and key informants,
the timing of annual rainfall in the MRD has shifted
with out-of-season rain, late onset of rainfall, and
increased rainfall at the end of the rainy season.
The number of storms and tropical depressions has
increased from five to ten per year. Heavy rain and
floods (caused by high tides) also occurred more
frequently in recent years. Figure 7 shows the results
of a participatory workshop on the most important

Saline intrusion and drought occur from November
to May, with intensity and frequency changing,
especially during El Niño years. The peak time for
salinity and drought is often from January to April.
According to the salinization map by the Centre for
Hydro-Meteorology Forecasting (NHCMF) (Figure 8),
the most serious salinization and drought happened
in the MRD during the periods of 2015-2016 and
2019-2020. The exposed areas are mainly located
in coastal provinces, including Ca Mau, Kien Giang,
Tra Vinh, Tien Giang, Ben Tre, Soc Trang, and Bac
Lieu. Ca Mau is the most affected province, with
90 percent of the province having experienced
a salinity rate of > 4g/L1 in dry years, followed by
the estuary provinces of Ben Tre, Bac Lieu, and Soc
Trang. In these provinces, saline conditions can
persist for more than 40 days annually, and up to 100
days in Ca Mau (Binh, Truong, and Long, 2020). The
impacts of salinization on agriculture, aquaculture,
and water access are significant. For example, in the
most severe dry season of 2015–2016, salinity levels
reached 30–40g/L in-field and 40–50g/L in shrimp
farms. Increased salinity severely impacted 53,000
ha of shrimp farms in Ca Mau (Ca Mau DARD, 2018).
In Ben Tre Province, 1,784 ha of aquaculture were
impacted, generating an economic loss of 1794
billion VND (74 million USD) for the agricultural
sector (Ben Tre DARD, 2017). This also has important
social implications, for example in terms of labor
migration. For instance, as agricultural activities are
postponed due to salinization or become ineffective
due to higher costs, many farmers in the MRD
have chosen to migrate to urban areas in search of
additional income (Be et al., 2017).

Heavy rain, prolonged rain, and floods are
experienced from June to November. High tides
occur from September to December. Coastal
flooding in the MRD has become increasingly
frequent and severe in recent years due to the
combined impacts of climate-change-induced
sea level rise and intense rainfall, along with
land subsidence, mangrove destruction, and
infrastructure construction (GIZ, 2013). The flooding
area is projected to grow, with the largest growth in
the provinces on the peninsula and its estuaries. In
a 2050 scenario, with high climate change effects
coupled with low hydropower and land-use change,
the provincial area flooded in Ca Mau and Kien
Giang is expected to increase by 84 percent and 76
percent respectively, while Ben Tre, Tra Vinh, and
Tien Giang will experience a 64–67 percent increase
in their flooded areas (MARD, 2016) (Figure 9).
Shrimp farming will be particularly impacted due
to changes in water quantity and quality (Ca Mau
DARD, 2018). Besides direct losses from shrimp or
fish being washed away, flooding damages farm
infrastructure, including pond dikes and sluice gates.
This allows polluted water to penetrate the ponds,
changing the water quality and pond salinity and
contributing to disease transmission (ADB, 2011).
Other conditions such as changes in seasonality,
high temperatures associated with high humidity, or
extreme events are conducive to pest and disease
outbreaks.
Overall, rice-shrimp farmers are more vulnerable
to climate change because these farmers and their
farming systems are located nearer to the coastal
areas which experience stronger impacts from
sea level rise, coastal erosion, saline intrusion, and
land subsidence associated with ground-water
over-extraction. Farmers living in the upper part
and in some of the middle part of the MRD have
more favorable conditions, such as having fertile
soils, year-round availability of freshwater, effective
irrigation and drainage systems, semi-flood depth,
or lack of salinity intrusion. These farmers have
higher capacity than those in lesser favorable zones.
Rural households with large farms usually have
higher resilience to natural and socio-economic
shocks than those with smaller farms (MARD, 2016).
While smallholder farmers in general are at risk,
ethnic minorities are reported as particularly
vulnerable to climate change. Khmer people are
one of the most dominant ethnic groups in the
MRD, with over 63 % living in the Soc Trang and
Tra Vinh provinces. They are some of the poorest
households in the MRD. Their low adaptive capacity,
low educational level, and low access to agricultural
services make them less able to cope with current
climate variability. They are living with poor water
sanitation and relying on untreated groundwater
for drinking, and their livelihoods strongly depend
on natural resources. Ethnic minority groups
are experiencing more economic losses due to
salinization and droughts in the early rainy season,
and pollution of water causing high mortality rates
of shrimp. The Khmer ethnic groups are further
disadvantaged as extension services for agriculture
and shrimp farming are often provided only in the
Vietnamese language (MARD, 2016).
Additionally, women are particularly vulnerable and
impacted more than men due to limited access to
technical services and credit, lack of land use rights,
and restricted decision-making in the family (Truong
et al., 2015, Tran, 2019). More men than women are
invited by extension workers or other agricultural
staff to participate in agricultural technical training or
meetings. As a result, women have limited adequate
extension services and technical information
about agricultural practices to adapt to climate
variability (Tran, 2019). Also, men are more likely
to communicate and seek farming advice from
extension workers or agricultural staff, whereas
women often get information through television
village loudspeakers, radio, leaflets, experienced
old men, and other women and neighbours in their
village (Truong et al., 2015). Moreover, women are
more dependent on men for decision-making,
resulting in limited opportunities to adopt new
agricultural technologies to adapt to climate change.
Particularly in rice-shrimp production, men still
play a vital role in conducting works and making
decisions in production. According to a study
conducted by (Tu, 2021) in Bac Lieu, Ca Mau, and
Soc Trang provinces, in more than 70% of surveyed
households, husbands are the ones to make
decisions related to production activities, only in 10-
20% of households do couples discuss with each
other before making a decision.
High levels of labor migration are occurring in
coastal areas due to climate risks, low education,
underdeveloped infrastructure, and lack of
economic opportunities (MARD, 2016; Tran et
al., 2023). Migration can be undertaken by young
people and more prosperous households for
employment and economic opportunities (MARD,
2016). Seasonal and temporary migration is often
taken by men (Grassi, Paris and Truong, 2017).
Migration can be a good adaptation strategy for
climate risks, but it can also increase vulnerability for
both migrants and those staying home by increasing
the amount of work for women left behind, including
dealing with hazards (MARD, 2016). Additionally, as
men still play the main role in rice-shrimp production
(Dang, 2020; Tu, 2021), this migration trend will leave
women with more responsibility in farming activities.
While this change has the potential to empower
women in agricultural production and decision
making, it also has the potential to put women at risk
if they lack the ability to adapt to change. Therefore,
it is critical to provide women with the necessary
support and resources to utilize these changes.
Investing in training programs and providing women
with new skills can equip them with the necessary
knowledge to fully realize potential benefits and
minimize risks.


Climate information
Climate information is needed by all actors to plan
activities and make decisions on how to best avoid
and minimize losses and damages from climate
hazards. The foremost needs are a three- to five-day
weather forecast (temperature and rainfall), early
warnings (heavy rain, floods or storms, drought,
salinization, etc.), disease outbreak alerts, and
seasonal forecasts (e.g., rainfall, temperature,
salinization). Table 3 provides an overview of the
existing climate services and needs of actors along
the rice-shrimp value chain. Television and radio
are the preferred delivery channels for most actors
along the value chain. However, producers (farmers)
also prefer other channels such as Zalo (messaging
App), loudspeaker, and governmental staff. This
shows high potential to promote the use of ICT in
climate information services to provide quick and
timely information, while also utilizing existing
channels to reach wider groups of users.

Climate services
The current climate information available to all
actors includes short-term weather forecasts, early
warnings and pest and disease alerts (Table 4).
However, farmers want access to more types of
information, with additional information on seasonal
and monthly forecasts and warnings. It may be
because they are more interested in these types
of information than the other actors. However, the
available information is still general and often covers
large geographical scales like national, regional,
and provincial levels. As a result, available climate
information services still do not meet the needs of
the value chain actors. Based on the key informant
interviews, most actors require more frequent and
precise forecasts for specific areas (e.g., district and
commune). Farmers require additional technical
training and advice on climate change adaptation
measures and improved reciprocal communication.
