According to Viet Nam’s National Strategy for
Natural Disaster Prevention, Response and
Mitigation to 2030, Vision to 2050, storms, floods,
droughts, and saltwater intrusion were identified as
national priorities (Vietnam’s Prime Minister, 2021).
These hazards also strongly affect agricultural
production. The most prominent issues in the MRD
are sea level rise, increased rainfall, extreme events
(e.g., storms, heavy rain, drought, floods), increased
temperatures, and salinity intrusion (CGIAR, 2016).
Because of sea level rise and climate change, the
MRD is experiencing more severe, longer lasting,
and more frequent droughts and more severe and
frequent salinity intrusion during the dry season.
According to agricultural experts and key informants,
the timing of annual rainfall in the MRD has shifted
with out-of-season rain, late onset of rainfall, and
increased rainfall at the end of the rainy season.
The number of storms and tropical depressions has
increased from five to ten per year. Heavy rain and
floods (caused by high tides) also occurred more
frequently in recent years. Figure 7 shows the results
of a participatory workshop on the most important
climate risks with significant impacts on agricultural
production in the MRD and their occurrence during
the year.

Saline intrusion and drought occur from November
to May, with intensity and frequency changing,
especially during El Niño years. The peak time for
salinity and drought is often from January to April.
According to the salinization map by the Centre for
Hydro-Meteorology Forecasting (NHCMF) (Figure 8),
the most serious salinization and drought happened
in the MRD during the periods of 2015-2016 and
2019-2020. The exposed areas are mainly located
in coastal provinces, including Ca Mau, Kien Giang,
Tra Vinh, Tien Giang, Ben Tre, Soc Trang, and Bac
Lieu. Ca Mau is the most affected province, with
90 percent of the province having experienced
a salinity rate of > 4g/L1 in dry years, followed by
the estuary provinces of Ben Tre, Bac Lieu, and Soc
Trang. In these provinces, saline conditions can
persist for more than 40 days annually, and up to 100
days in Ca Mau (Binh, Truong, and Long, 2020). The
impacts of salinization on agriculture, aquaculture,
and water access are significant. For example, in the
most severe dry season of 2015–2016, salinity levels
reached 30–40g/L in-field and 40–50g/L in shrimp
farms. Increased salinity severely impacted 53,000
ha of shrimp farms in Ca Mau (Ca Mau DARD, 2018).
In Ben Tre Province, 1,784 ha of aquaculture were
impacted, generating an economic loss of 1794
billion VND (74 million USD) for the agricultural
sector (Ben Tre DARD, 2017). This also has important
social implications, for example in terms of labor
migration. For instance, as agricultural activities are
postponed due to salinization or become ineffective
due to higher costs, many farmers in the MRD
have chosen to migrate to urban areas in search of
additional income (Be et al., 2017).

Heavy rain, prolonged rain, and floods are
experienced from June to November. High tides
occur from September to December. Coastal
flooding in the MRD has become increasingly
frequent and severe in recent years due to the
combined impacts of climate-change-induced
sea level rise and intense rainfall, along with
land subsidence, mangrove destruction, and
infrastructure construction (GIZ, 2013). The flooding
area is projected to grow, with the largest growth in
the provinces on the peninsula and its estuaries. In
a 2050 scenario, with high climate change effects
coupled with low hydropower and land-use change,
the provincial area flooded in Ca Mau and Kien
Giang is expected to increase by 84 percent and 76
percent respectively, while Ben Tre, Tra Vinh, and
Tien Giang will experience a 64–67 percent increase
in their flooded areas (MARD, 2016) (Figure 9).
Shrimp farming will be particularly impacted due
to changes in water quantity and quality (Ca Mau
DARD, 2018). Besides direct losses from shrimp or
fish being washed away, flooding damages farm
infrastructure, including pond dikes and sluice gates.
This allows polluted water to penetrate the ponds,
changing the water quality and pond salinity and
contributing to disease transmission (ADB, 2011).
Other conditions such as changes in seasonality,
high temperatures associated with high humidity, or
extreme events are conducive to pest and disease
outbreaks.
Overall, rice-shrimp farmers are more vulnerable
to climate change because these farmers and their
farming systems are located nearer to the coastal
areas which experience stronger impacts from
sea level rise, coastal erosion, saline intrusion, and
land subsidence associated with ground-water
over-extraction. Farmers living in the upper part
and in some of the middle part of the MRD have
more favorable conditions, such as having fertile
soils, year-round availability of freshwater, effective
irrigation and drainage systems, semi-flood depth,
or lack of salinity intrusion. These farmers have
higher capacity than those in lesser favorable zones.
Rural households with large farms usually have
higher resilience to natural and socio-economic
shocks than those with smaller farms (MARD, 2016).
While smallholder farmers in general are at risk,
ethnic minorities are reported as particularly
vulnerable to climate change. Khmer people are
one of the most dominant ethnic groups in the
MRD, with over 63 % living in the Soc Trang and
Tra Vinh provinces. They are some of the poorest
households in the MRD. Their low adaptive capacity,
low educational level, and low access to agricultural
services make them less able to cope with current
climate variability. They are living with poor water
sanitation and relying on untreated groundwater
for drinking, and their livelihoods strongly depend
on natural resources. Ethnic minority groups
are experiencing more economic losses due to
salinization and droughts in the early rainy season,
and pollution of water causing high mortality rates
of shrimp. The Khmer ethnic groups are further
disadvantaged as extension services for agriculture
and shrimp farming are often provided only in the
Vietnamese language (MARD, 2016).
Additionally, women are particularly vulnerable and
impacted more than men due to limited access to
technical services and credit, lack of land use rights,
and restricted decision-making in the family (Truong
et al., 2015, Tran, 2019). More men than women are
invited by extension workers or other agricultural
staff to participate in agricultural technical training or
meetings. As a result, women have limited adequate
extension services and technical information
about agricultural practices to adapt to climate
variability (Tran, 2019). Also, men are more likely
to communicate and seek farming advice from
extension workers or agricultural staff, whereas
women often get information through television
village loudspeakers, radio, leaflets, experienced
old men, and other women and neighbours in their
village (Truong et al., 2015). Moreover, women are
more dependent on men for decision-making,
resulting in limited opportunities to adopt new
agricultural technologies to adapt to climate change.
Particularly in rice-shrimp production, men still
play a vital role in conducting works and making
decisions in production. According to a study
conducted by (Tu, 2021) in Bac Lieu, Ca Mau, and
Soc Trang provinces, in more than 70% of surveyed
households, husbands are the ones to make
decisions related to production activities, only in 10-
20% of households do couples discuss with each
other before making a decision.
High levels of labor migration are occurring in
coastal areas due to climate risks, low education,
underdeveloped infrastructure, and lack of
economic opportunities (MARD, 2016; Tran et
al., 2023). Migration can be undertaken by young
people and more prosperous households for
employment and economic opportunities (MARD,
2016). Seasonal and temporary migration is often
taken by men (Grassi, Paris and Truong, 2017).
Migration can be a good adaptation strategy for
climate risks, but it can also increase vulnerability for
both migrants and those staying home by increasing
the amount of work for women left behind, including
dealing with hazards (MARD, 2016). Additionally, as
men still play the main role in rice-shrimp production
(Dang, 2020; Tu, 2021), this migration trend will leave
women with more responsibility in farming activities.
While this change has the potential to empower
women in agricultural production and decision
making, it also has the potential to put women at risk
if they lack the ability to adapt to change. Therefore,
it is critical to provide women with the necessary
support and resources to utilize these changes.
Investing in training programs and providing women
with new skills can equip them with the necessary
knowledge to fully realize potential benefits and
minimize risks.

1.1. Short background
As part of the CGIAR Initiative on Asian Mega-Deltas,
a scoping study was completed by the end of 2022
to prioritize key delta-oriented production systems
and value chains in Viet Nam's Mekong River Delta
(MRD) (Gonsalves et al., 2022). Rice, rice-shrimp,
and fruit trees production systems and value chains
were selected based on a set of criteria including
production, economic and nutritional value, climate
change and impact, and potential benefit of climate
services with specific attention for the role of women
and smallholders. To better understand the selected
commodity production systems in the MRD, an
additional value chain climate risk and vulnerability
assessment was made. This report focuses on rice
production and its value chain.
1.2. Rationale and justification
for the study
Viet Nam is one of the three biggest rice exporters
in the world. In 2021, the country produced around
43.9 million metric tons of rice, which showed
a modest growth of about 2.6 percent over the
previous year. Rice farming in the MRD accounts for
more than half of the total rice production in Viet
Nam and more than 95 percent of total rice exports
(World Bank, 2022). However, the rice production
area in the MRD has been decreasing recently
(Figure 1), due to the impacts of climate variability
and changes, upstream water management, and
anthropogenic activities (Nguyen et al., 2022).
Recent governmental policy (Decision 287/QĐ-TTg,
2020) allowed farmers to change “ineffective” rice
production into fruits and aquaculture.
According to the Mekong Delta Economic Report
2022 (VCCI & Fulbright, 2022), environmental
challenges of agricultural production in MRD
include: (1) the impacts from the operation of
upstream hydroelectric dams and the freshwater
system for agriculture in Thailand and Cambodia that
lead to a decreased amount of silt, soil erosion, water
shortage, and salinity, making it difficult for farmers
to plan crop management throughout the season;
and (2) the declined quality of cropping land which
is affecting the agricultural production in the area.
The most common climatic hazards that affect rice
production in the MRD are inundation and saline
intrusion associated with sea level rise, pest and
disease outbreaks, changes in temperature, and the
frequency and intensity of heavy rain, flooding, and
storms (Tung & Higano, 2011). This has led to and
will continue to result in a significant reduction in rice
yield and rice income for small-holder farmers (Jiang
et al., 2018). For example, the Ministry of Agriculture
and Rural Development (MARD) reported in May
2016 that the El Niño event in that year had caused
severe salinization and drought and a reduction
in rice production of 1.1 million tonnes in the MRD
(FAO, 2016), one of the highest nationally recorded
losses. Land degradation and soil loss due to sea
level rise, drought, and flooding also contributed to
a reduction in rice production area (Kai et al., 2021).
Climate variability and changes not only threaten
the production of rice directly, but also affect other
stages of the rice value chain, such as harvesting,
transportation, processing, storage, and marketing.
This leads to serious losses in revenue and income
for the actors along the value chain. For instance, a
reduction in crop yield and quality due to drought
and saline intrusion leads to a shortage of product
supply for traders and other related actors. Heavy
rainfall during the harvesting period reduces the
quality of the product, increases harvesting and
transportation costs, and makes it difficult to process
and store rice. This further reduces market value
and results in higher costs for farmers. The risks
are expected to increase in the coming years with
a significant impact on rice yields in the MRD in the
future (Nguyen & Hoang, 2015).
Therefore, access to climate information services
plays an important role for value chain actors to
adapt and reduce the impacts of climate variability
and change. This report aims to understand
the current situation of rice production and the
value chain, and how value chain actors can be
better supported with climate information and
complementary services.

1.3. Purpose and approach
This report aims to study the rice production and
value chain in the provinces of the MRD to gain a
deeper understanding of:
1) General characteristics and relative importance of
the production system and value chain across the
delta
2) Value chain components, their relations, and key
actors
3) Climate risks, vulnerabilities, and impacts across
the value chain
4) Climate information and complementary services
5) Enabling environment (institutions, policy, etc.)
The initial assessment was conducted from February
2023 to April 2023 and updated from September
to October 2023. The assessment used a variety
of methodologies to gain a better understanding
of the rice production system and value chain, the
climate risks and vulnerability, and the enabling
environment. This included a review of secondary
data, reports, and information from previous projects
and literature. To further explore the role of current
climate information and complementary services
and gaps, a focus group discussion was included
with seven local agricultural officers at district and
provincial level, and key informant interviews with
relevant stakeholder groups (three input suppliers,
two traders, one export company, and one retailer).
The report was reviewed by an agricultural and rice
value chain expert.
The production system and
value chain
2.1. The rice production system
in the Mekong River Delta
In 2020, the MRD provinces cultivated a total of
3,963.7 hectares of land for rice cultivation, which
was 54.5 percent of the entire rice-cultivated area
in the country (GSO, 2022). Kien Giang, An Giang,
and Dong Thap provinces are major contributors
to regional rice production, accounting for nearly
half of the region's rice production (Figure 2). There
are typically two to three rice crops cultivated per
year. Winter-spring rice has the highest yield of the
three main rice crops, accounting for 44 percent
of total rice production for the year. The winterspring production is critical to the success of both
regional and national rice production. For example,
in 2021, the MRD produced 10.94 million tons of
winter-spring rice, an increase of 365.5 thousand
tons over the previous winter-spring crop, and
accounting for 53.2 percent of the country's winterspring rice production (GSO, 2022). Rice quality is
also improving, with speciality rice varieties such
as Jasmine 85, VD20, OM5451, OM6976, OM18,
OM7347, OM4900, and ST varieties for export
increasing in both area and quantity, which increases
market competitiveness
According to Bui et al. (2018), rice cultivation in the
MRD has been experiencing several transformations
in the past three decades due to government
policies on irrigation expansion, export markets,
technological advances, environment and climate
change, and urbanization and industrialization.
Changes included:
i) An increase from 1-2 crops per year to 3 main
crops per year during the 1990s.
ii) A transformation during the 2000s from 2-3
high-yield rice crops into 1 rice crop and
1-2 rotated cash crops (i.e., vegetables and
corn) in some provinces in the Long Xuyen
Quadrangular, the Middle zone between Tien
and Hau rivers, and the East Coastal Estuaries
(An Giang, Vinh Long, Soc Trang).
iii) A transformation from 2-3 rice crops to mixed
rice and aquaculture farming or rotational rice
and aquaculture farming in the coastal areas
of provinces in the East Coastal Estuaries and
Ca Mau Peninsula, especially since 2015 as
indicated in the Decree 35/2015/ND-CP on
management and use of rice land areas.
iv) Over the last 15 years, a shift from 2-3 high-yield
rice crops to fruit trees in Dong Thap Muoi, the
Middle Zone between Tien and Hau rivers, and
the East Coastal Estuaries, including upstream
districts.
Recent years also observed a change in land use
from rice to perennial crops and aquaculture
(Nguyen et al., 2022), especially in the “ineffective
rice” areas impacted by saline instruction, drought
heavy rain, and flooding. From 2001 to 2015, the
total rice cultivation area increased by 579 thousand
ha as the result of intensive development of triple
rice cropping in the upper zone (Figure 3). However,
in 2020 the rice area decreased by 380 thousand
ha (Vu, et al., 2022). At the same time, aquaculture
areas dramatically increased by more than 2400
km2 in 2020 and the area for perennial crops nearly
doubled between 2005 to 2020 (Nguyen et al.,
2022)

In the recent update on sustainable development
planning for the MRD by the Viet Nam Prime Minister
(Decision 287/QĐ-TTg 2022), the MRD is divided
into three main ecological zones (Figure 4):
• Freshwater zone in the upstream and central
MRD, the key area for sustainable rice, fruits,
and freshwater aquaculture development,
and flood control for the MRD. This covers five
provinces (An Giang, Đong Thap, Hau Giang,
Vinh Long, Can Tho) and parts of six other
provinces (Kien Giang, Soc Trang, Tra Vinh, Ben
Tre, Tien Giang, and Long An).
• Brackish water zone in the middle of the delta,
prioritized for fruit trees, together with highquality rice, freshwater fisheries, vegetables,
and industrial crops (i.e. sugarcanes, coconuts,
etc.). This zone includes parts of eight provinces
(Kien Giang, Ca Mau, Bac Lieu, Soc Trang, Tra
Vinh, Ben Tre, Tien Giang, and Long An).
• Saline water zone for developing salt and
brackish water aquaculture, rice intercropped
or rotated with aquaculture and agroforestry
systems, conservation of mangrove forest,
and response to natural disasters and coastal
erosion. This zone covers parts of eight
provinces (Kien Giang, Ca Mau, Bac Lieu, Soc
Trang, Tra Vinh, Ben Tre, Tien Giang and Long
An).
Rice is cultivated in all three zones, with most of the
cultivated area in the freshwater zone, followed
by the brackish zone and then the saline water
zone. The government encourages changing
the ineffective rice production areas to fruits and
aquaculture, including the rice-shrimp system, to
better adapt to climate change and improve farmers’
income (Decision 287/QĐ-TTg, 2022)

2.2. Key components of the rice
value chain and their relations
In Viet Nam’s MRD, rice value chains generally
include five components: (1) input supply; (2)
production; (3) trading; (4) processing; and (5)
marketing. The two most common typologies
based on consumption channels are the domestic
rice value chain and the export rice value chain (Vo
& Nguyen, 2011; Demont & Rutsaert, 2017; Dao,
et al., 2020) (Figure 5). The domestic rice value
chain accounts for 30 percent of the region's rice
production. Rice is obtained by wholesalers and
retailers from three sources:
i) Traders who buy paddy from farmers and have
it milled for local consumption, primarily in the
vicinity of the mill, accounting for one-third of
rice supplied to domestic consumers.
ii) Mills that supply rice for urban areas (town,
cities, suburbs)
iii) Large milling and polishing firms that supply
cities
The export rice value chain accounts for 70 percent
of rice production in the region. According to Dao et
al. (2020), this consists of three channels:
i) A direct channel in which farmers sell paddy
for milling and polishing directly to exporting
firms, accounting for a small fraction of the total
exports.
ii) A two-tier channel, which the majority of rice
produced will go through, in which farmers sell
paddy to traders who bring it to exporters for
milling and polishing.
iii) A three-tier channel in which traders sell
paddy to mills, who sell loose rice to exporters,
accounting for one-sixth of total exports

chain and their characteristics

farmers, traders, processors, wholesalers/retailers,

contribute to the value chain, such as extension
services, companies, local governments, the Ministry
of Agriculture and Rural Development, the Ministry
of Industry and Trade, customs, banks, institutes, and
universities (Vo & Nguyen, 2011).

Climate risks and
vulnerabilities
3.1. Key climate risks, vulnerabilities,
and impacts in the Mekong River Delta
The most prominent climate changes issues in
the MRD are sea level rise, increased rainfall,
increased number of extreme weather events (e.g.
storm, intense rain, drought, floods), increased
temperatures, and salinity intrusion (CGIAR, 2016).
03 The MRD is geographically prone to experience
excessive and intense rainfall, floods, and storms
from June to November, as well as saline intrusion
and drought from November to May, peaking
during January to April (Figure 6). These natural
disasters affect the rice production area, rice yield,
and other aspects of farmer livelihood in the region.
Nguyen and Hoang (2015) estimate that, due to the
decrease in rainfall, the reduction in rice productivity
is greatest for spring paddy, followed by winter,
autumn-winter, and autumn paddy. The interviews
and expert consultation revealed that increased
temperature and humidity are conducive to pest and
disease outbreaks throughout the season and cause
problems for product storage.
Nguyen and Hoang (2015) show that the most
vulnerable provinces, Ca Mau and Tra Vinh, are
located in the southern part of Viet Nam (Figure 7).
Both provinces have low elevation and a high rate
of rain-fed rice, while experiencing more extreme
rainfall and temperatures. Bac Lieu, Soc Trang,
and Ben Tre provinces are moderately vulnerable,
affected by sea level rise. An Giang and Dong Thap
provinces have the lowest vulnerability, with less
extreme fluctuations in rainfall, low susceptibility to
sea level rise, and they do not have any rain-fed rice
area
Recent simulation modelling of paddy land
under different scenarios (Representative
Concentration Pathways - RCP 4.5 and RCP 8.5)
projected a substantial decrease in land suitable
for rice cultivation (Nguyen & Hoang, 2015), with an
estimated 76.6 percent of land becoming unsuitable
for rice by 2050 (Dang et al., 2020). Livelihoods in
coastal provinces are more vulnerable than those in
the middle plains. As a result, adaptation to climate
change and environmental pressures is often more
difficult in these areas, as small coastal farmers are
usually unable or unwilling to expand their growing
areas, often due to a lack of experience or fear of
disaster risks (Tran et al., 2022).
Besides demographic factors, rice farmers’
vulnerability is affected by factors such as weather
information, flood occurrence, drought occurrence,
access to extension services, access to credit,
and cooperative membership (Diem et al., 2021).
Smallholder farmers, in general, face risks associated
with climate change, but ethnic minorities and
women are particularly susceptible. Within the
MRD, 43 distinct ethnic minorities constitute
7.6% of the total population with Khmer being
the most dominant and living mainly in Tra Vinh
and Soc Trang province (Tra, 2023). Despite their

Women are similarly disadvantaged. They are more
vulnerable than men, due to their lack of access
to inputs and lower wages, which results in less
participation in the rice value chain in the MRD
(GIC, 2021). Women also face other structural and
institutional obstacles such as limited land-use rights,
restricted access to formal credits, and exclusion
from agricultural extension programs (Truong et al,
2010). As a result, women encounter a deficiency in
both technical information and investment needed
to navigate climate variability, leading to increased
vulnerability and more pronounced impacts
compared to their male counterparts (Truong et
al., 2015). Task allocation reveals distinct roles, with
men predominantly serving as the primary decisionmakers in production, while women assume
significant responsibilities in trading and marketing
(ACIAR, 2019). Despite being entrusted with the
responsibility of managing household income,
women still experience a power imbalance, as men
retain greater influence over decisions, particularly
those crucial to changing farming techniques (Tran,
2019).

3.2. Climate risks, impacts,
and adaptation measures
across the rice value chain
Climate hazards can either directly impact certain
value chain components or can impact indirectly
through effects on other components and actors.
Table 2 summarizes the key climate risk, impacts, and
current adaptation measures along the rice value
chain
Overall, the rice value chain is influenced directly
and most significantly by rainfall (intensity, on-set,
amount) and salinization associated with drought,
followed by other hazards. Heavy rain, storms, saline
intrusion, and drought are the most common climate
risks in the MRD (Table 2).
Rice farmers are the most vulnerable actors in the
value chain. They benefit least from value added
along the value chain, while they are most exposed
and at risk of climate hazards, such as sea level rise
causing inundation and saline intrusion, disease
outbreaks, heavy rain, abnormal flooding, increased
extreme weather events, and abnormal temperature
changes (Tung & Higano, 2011). Rainfall, drought,
and salinization are important factors, influencing
critical agricultural decisions like the number of
rice seasons per year, type of farming systems (e.g.,
changing rice-rice to rice-shrimp), rice varieties,
and the farming calendar, which greatly affect the
production stage and subsequently affect market
prices. Heavy rain, flooding, and storms can cause
sudden loss of production at the harvesting stage.
Higher temperatures, heavy rainfall, and humidity
increase the risk of pest and disease outbreaks
throughout the season. Farmers have recently
deployed adaptation strategies such as: 1) adjusting
crop calendars and stop planting in case of drought,
as per official advice (CGIAR, 2016); 2) rotating with
shorter-term crops, or converting rice land to grow
other crops or aquaculture (Toan et al., 2021); 3)
using updated information on irrigation system
operations (e.g. opening and closing of sluice gates)
for improved storage and use of freshwater; and 4)
adapting plans and management practices based on
weather forecasts (pest and disease management,
planting dates, harvesting dates, etc.). Additionally,
farmers' practices have shifted from using large
quantities of fertilizers and pesticides to more
sustainable production and better adaptation to
climate change. Some notable practices that are

Other actors are affected both directly and indirectly
by different climate hazards. Heavy rain, flooding,
and storms can disrupt value chain activities such
as transportation and product storage. Traders and
input providers in the MRD are more vulnerable to
these risks when transporting paddy/rice by boat,
due to a lack of ability to manage risks compared to
exporters and wholesalers/retailers (Vo & Nguyen,
2011). Higher temperatures, heavy rainfall, and
humidity affect other actors in the value chain, such
as processors, exporters, and wholesale/retail
stores. These actors are also indirectly impacted
by the failure of rice production and shifts from
rice cultivation into other production systems. Low
production increases the risks of not having enough
product supply for other actors, while changing
production systems affect the rice supply and the
inputs that can be traded by the input providers
in the locality. These actors usually have goodcondition facilities in place, which are capable
of managing these risks if they respond to daily
and long-term weather forecasts and hydrology
information and provide better support for farmers
in terms of technical advice, accessibility to weather
forecasts, and risk management plans.
Climate information and
complementary services
The tables below describe the climate information
needs of different actors along the value chain and
the existing services and gaps. They are the result
of a focus group discussion and key informant
interviews with a range of stakeholders from
provinces in the MRD.
4.1. Climate information needs
Different actors in the value chain share a common
need for short-term weather forecasting – threeto ten-day forecasts throughout the season, with
essential variables such as rainfall amount and
temperature ranges, and warnings for risks of
extreme weather such as storms, heavy rainfall,
floods, and drought (Table 3). Early extreme weather
warning is very important to farmers throughout the
season, but mostly at the harvest time for traders
and wholesalers/retailers to proactively plan and
avoid supply source scarcity. For short-term periods
during the season, especially during periods with
high risk of salinity, farmers also need to be updated
on a daily basis with information on irrigation system
operations, so they may manage water for irrigation
and mitigate the impact of saline intrusion.
On the other hand, long-term forecasts, such
as seasonal forecasts from three to six months,
especially forecasting the start of rainfall season,
rainfall amount, and risks of drought and salinization,
are requested by input suppliers, processors, and
farmers, who before starting the season need to
foresee the requirements for seed varieties and
input materials and to prepare adaptative measures
for any adverse events.
Other climate-related information, such as crop
damage reports, were mentioned by traders and
exporters, to assess total productivity, so they may
negotiate more reasonable purchases and contracts,
as well as to avoid supply source scarcity
