# Chapter 1: Introduction
•
- This chapter
  - sets the stage by highlighting the increased importance of prediction in modern society
    - particularly in the post-war era
- It emphasizes that
  - **prediction** is not merely a technical exercise
  - but is embedded in
    - social
    - cultural
    - and political contexts.
•
- The chapter introduces the concept of "cultures of prediction
  - " arguing that the post-war period has seen a significant expansion in the social impact of predictive practices. These practices are linked to new technologies and cultural interests, taking on new forms and meanings.
•
It identifies a trend toward state planning and intervention, particularly after crises like World War I and the Great Depression. This led to an increased need for "investigation of futures" and expertise in areas such as demography, welfare, and economics.
•
The chapter notes that the atmospheric sciences provide a powerful example for the study of cultures of prediction. It also indicates that the development of predictive practices was not straightforward, but rather dependent on historical circumstances, involving competitions and conflicts between different scientific approaches.
•
The chapter mentions a shift in climate modeling from being a heuristic tool to a predictive one in the 1970s, which strengthened concerns about climate warming.
