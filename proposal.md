- This study does not claim to examine every climate adaptation intervention in the Mekong Delta. Rather, I used a two-step selection strategy. First, I systematically scanned 47 Vietnamese-language news articles, project documents, and policy reports (2015–2024) to identify recurrent adaptation practices. Second, I grouped emergent interventions by their underlying logic of action. This process yielded five analytically distinct categories: genetic (salt-tolerant seeds), hydrological-informational (digital advisory apps), temporal-coordinative (planting calendars), epistemic-cultural (indigenous knowledge), and political-economic (policy frameworks). Other interventions, such as weather-index insurance or shrimp-rice rotation, either operate through these same logics (insurance as a policy-economic instrument) or address post-harvest dynamics beyond this study’s farm-level focus. The five objects thus represent a typologically saturated set: each occupies a non-redundant dimension of adaptation, and together they capture the dominant ways climate resilience is being materialized, governed, and contested in the contemporary Mekong Delta.”

  - These five objects – policy frameworks, salt‑tolerant seeds, planting calendars, digital apps, and indigenous knowledge – are not a random grab‑bag. They were selected because they represent distinct timescales of adaptation, an emerging analytical lens in climate vulnerability research (Tran et al., 2019; Barnett, 2020). Each object operates on a different temporal horizon: policy works across decades, seed breeding across 5–10 years, planting calendars across a single season, digital apps in real time, and indigenous knowledge across generations. I argue that climate adaptation in the Mekong Delta is not simply a matter of adding more tools; it is a problem of temporal coordination among these mismatched rhythms. By ordering the analysis from the longest to the shortest timescale – from policy to indigenous knowledge – the study reveals how lags, frictions, and unintended opportunities emerge when decadal plans meet weekly salinity pulses, or when generational heuristics are destabilised by upstream dams. This timescale continuum provides the logical spine for the entire investigation. The five objects are not at the same “level”, but that is precisely the point: adaptation requires bridging across levels of time, and breakdowns occur where these levels fail to align.
