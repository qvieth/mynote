# culture of prediction

- about the authors:

## 1 introduction - written by 3 authors
- Prediction influences every aspect of our lives, as social actions and decisions—whether casual or complex—are guided by expectations about the future and the outcomes of our interactions.



## Chapter 1
- introduces the book's focus on cultures of prediction in the environmental, predominantly atmospheric, sciences, which are representative of other environmental research domains

- The book aims to explore:
  - what cultures of prediction are
  - how they've formed
  - how they function
  - and how they differ

- It also seeks to understand:
  - how predictive knowledge about the environment is
    - produced
    - validated
    - and used
    - as well as how inherent uncertainties are **managed**

- Further, it investigates how these cultures of prediction have:
  - gained legitimacy and authority in both science and broader culture
  - and how they interact with society

- The chapter also notes the book will examine:
  - how these cultures of prediction emerged through dialogue with broader society
  - and inform
  - and shape social perceptions
    - meanings
    - and practices

## Chapter 2
- delves **deeper** into the concept of "cultures of prediction,"

- placing their emergence
  - based on computer modeling and simulation
  - in a broader historical perspective

- It argues that
  - while prediction has always been a feature of human societies
    - the **post-war era** was
      - crucial for the **development**
      - and **increased** social impact of these cultures

- Predictive knowledge **production** in this era
  - utilized new technologies
  - served **new** cultural interests
  - and assumed new forms, roles, and meanings

- The **atmospheric sciences** are
  - presented as a **key example** for studying the **features** of cultures of prediction

- The chapter further outlines **5 key dimensions** for understanding cultures of prediction:
  - The **social role** of prediction.
  - The **character and significance** of computational practices.
  - The **domestication of uncertainty.**
  - The **degree of institutionalization and professionalization** of predictive expertise.
  - The **cultural impact** of predictive practices and claims.

- The chapter argues that
  - prediction is always **pursued for a social purpose**
    - which can be either to
      - advance science
      - or to **advance policy,**
        - with the book primarily focusing on the latter

- It also discusses how computational practices
  - the use of
    - apparatus
    - devices
    - machines
    - and systems
    - structure
    - and constrain human perception
    - and action
